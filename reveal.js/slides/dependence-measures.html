\(\textit{5. Dependence Measures}\)
  • Whether two random variables move in the same direction or in opposite directions is essential in characterizing their dependence. Most dependence measures are based on this concept of \(\textit{concordance}\) and \(\textit{discordance}\).
\(\textbf{\textit{Def 5.1 (Concordance, Discordance): }}\) Let \((X, Y)\) be a continuous bivariate random vector with joint distribution \(F_{X, Y}\). Let \((X_1, Y_1)\), \((X_2, Y_2)\) be an independent sample from \(F_{X, Y}\). Then \((X_1, Y_1)\) and \((X_2, Y_2)\) are said to be concordant if \(X_1 < X_2\) and \(Y_1 < Y_2\) or if \(X_1> X_2\) and \(Y_1 > Y_2\). They are said to be discordant if \(X_1 < X_2\) and \(Y_1> Y_2\) or if \(X_1 > X_2\) and \(Y_1 < Y_2\).
\(\textbf{\textit{Def 5.2 (Kendall's Tau): }}\) Let \((X, Y)\) be a continuous bivariate random vector with joint distribution \(F_{X, Y}\). Let \((X_1, Y_1)\), \((X_2, Y_2)\) be an independent sample from \(F_{X,Y}\). Then Kendall's tau is defined as \[ \tau_{X, Y} = P[(X_1 - X_2)(Y_1 - Y_2) > 0] - P[(X_1 - X_2)(Y_1 - Y_2) < 0] \]
  • Note that \(-1 \leq \tau_{X, Y} \leq 1\), with positive values indicating \(X\) and \(Y\) tend to move in the same direction and negative values indicating they tend to move in opposite directions.
