\(\textit{2. Properties of Copulas}\)
  • Most properties of copulas follow from the fact that they describe joint distributions.
    1. 2.1. \(C_{X, Y}(u, v)\) is increasing in each argument
    2. 2.2. \(C_{X, Y}(0, v) = 0 = C_{X, Y}(u, 0)\)
    3. 2.3. \(C_{X, Y}(u, 1) = u\text{, } C_{X, Y}(1, v) = v\)
    4. 2.4. \(\max(u + v - 1, 0) \leq C_{X, Y}(u, v) \leq \min(u, v)\)
  • For all \(0 \leq u_1 \leq u_2 \leq 1\) and \(0 \leq v_1 \leq v_2 \leq 1\),
    1. 2.5. \([C_{X, Y}(u_2, v_2) - C_{X, Y}(u_1, v_2)] - [C_{X, Y}(u_2, v_1) - C_{X, Y}(u_1, v_1)] \geq 0\)
    2. 2.6. \(|C_{X, Y}(u_2, v_2) - C_{X, Y}(u_1, v_1)| \leq |u_2 - u_1| + |v_2 - v_1|\)
  • If \(X\) and \(Y\) are continuous and independent, then
    1. 2.7. \(C_{X, Y}(u, v) = F_{X, Y}(x, y) = u v\)
  • This is the product copula, denoted by \(C_{X, Y}(u, v) = \Pi\).
  • If \(X\) and \(Y\) are continuous random variables, and \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are strictly increasing functions on the support of \(X\) and \(Y\), then
    1. 2.8. \(C_{\alpha(X), \beta(Y)}(u, v) = C_{X, Y}(u, v)\)
  • This is known as the invariance property of copulas (under strictly increasing transformations).
